# Day One Lesson Plan — Introducing the AI

A 15-minute mini-lesson for the day you tell your students about the classroom AI experiment. Use this as a starting script; adapt to your class's tone and your real voice.

You don't need slides. You just need to talk to your kids.

---

## Goals for this lesson

By the end of the 15 minutes, your students should know:

1. What's happening (you'll be using an AI to help prepare class materials)
2. What that means for them (they won't talk to it; they'll see better stuff)
3. What it doesn't mean (it's not grading them, watching them, or replacing you)
4. They can opt out without penalty
5. They have real input — including (if applicable) shaping who the AI is

You should also walk away with a sense of who in the room is excited, who's skeptical, and who's confused. Those signals matter for how you adjust the next few weeks.

---

## The flow (15 minutes)

### Opening (2 minutes) — get their attention honestly

Don't bury the lede. Open with something like:

> "Hey — I want to tell you about something different we're going to try in this class. I'm going to start using an AI to help me prepare some of the stuff I make for you. Slides, the cards I hand out, the messages I send home. I want you to hear it from me before you see it."

Read the room. Some classes will react. Some won't. Either is fine.

### What this is (3 minutes) — the simple version

> "Here's the short version. I've installed an AI tool on my computer. It helps me prepare class materials faster and (I hope) better. It looks at general patterns in our class — how many people are caught up, what assignments are causing the most trouble — and helps me draft slides, encouragement notes, messages home.
>
> I review everything before you see it. The AI doesn't decide anything about you. It doesn't grade. It doesn't talk to you. It doesn't replace me. It's like having a really fast assistant who helps me prep — except the assistant is a computer program."

### What it does NOT do (3 minutes) — head off fears

This is the part that matters most. Be explicit.

> "Things this AI does NOT do:
> - **It does not see your name with your grades.** I use little tools on my computer to handle that stuff. The AI itself never sees who scored what. It sees things like 'twelve students are caught up this week' — never tied to a person.
> - **It does not read your writing or your messages.** I don't share that.
> - **It does not watch you.** It doesn't see cameras, microphones, or screen activity. None of that.
> - **It is not deciding anything about you.** I am.
> - **It is not replacing me.** I still teach this class. I still grade. I still email you back. The AI just helps me prep faster."

### What this might look like for you (2 minutes) — set expectations

> "What you might notice:
> - Probably better-looking handouts than I usually make
> - More frequent notes from me — usually good ones, like celebrations or check-ins
> - Themed slides
> - Sometimes printed cards listing what you're missing, with checkboxes — those are for you, not graded
> - If you do something cool, maybe a certificate"

### Their input matters (3 minutes) — choose one path below

**Path A — if you're letting students help shape the AI:**

> "Here's the part where you come in. I haven't named the AI yet. I haven't picked its personality yet. I want you to help decide.
>
> Tomorrow I'm going to give you a quick form. You'll suggest a name. You'll pick a personality. You'll tell me what you want the AI to focus on. The most-voted answers actually go in. This is real.
>
> Take it seriously. Take it fun. Both. We're going to be saying this name a lot — pick something that won't make you cringe in three weeks."

**Path B — if you've already named/shaped the AI:**

> "I've named the AI **[AI NAME]**. I picked the personality based on what I thought would work for this class — [trait], [trait], [trait]. If after a couple of weeks the voice feels off for our class, tell me and we'll adjust. This isn't fixed."

### Opt-out (1 minute) — make it real, not performative

> "Last thing: if you'd rather not be part of this — if it weirds you out, if your family says no, anything — you can opt out. Tell me directly, or have someone at home email me. You'll get the same stuff I'd have made without the AI. There's no penalty. Nothing about how I treat you in class changes. I won't make a big deal of it."

### Closing (1 minute) — invitation to push back

> "I'm going to be honest with you — I don't know exactly how this is going to go. I think it'll be good. But I'm trying it. If at any point you think it's making things worse instead of better, tell me. That's not me being polite. That's me asking for real feedback. The AI is a tool. We're going to use it for as long as it helps and stop when it doesn't.
>
> Questions?"

### Q&A (open-ended)

Take questions. Common ones and good answers:

> **"Is the AI grading us?"**
> No. I grade. The AI never sees a grade.

> **"Can it read what I write?"**
> No. Anything you write — essays, messages, anonymous surveys — I don't share with the AI unless we agree it's appropriate (like a class-wide anonymous survey where the AI helps me find themes — never quoting anyone). And we'd talk about that first.

> **"Why are you doing this?"**
> [Your honest reason. Don't oversell. Examples: "I'm trying to be better at noticing when one of you is quietly struggling." / "I want to send more notes home — good ones — and the AI helps me do that without it taking my whole evening."]

> **"What if I don't want my family contacted?"**
> Then we won't. Tell me and I'll make sure no AI-generated message goes to your family.

> **"Is this going to be on the test?"**
> No.

> **"Can WE use AI?"**
> Separate conversation. This project is about how I use AI to teach. Whether and how you use AI in your own work is a different policy — let's talk about it as it comes up.

> **"What if the AI says something mean or wrong?"**
> Tell me immediately. I review everything before it reaches you, so it shouldn't happen, but if it does — that's a real problem and I want to know.

## After the lesson

In your own notes, jot down:

- Which kids seemed excited
- Which seemed skeptical or uncomfortable
- Any specific questions that surprised you
- Any opt-out requests

You'll use these to adjust Week 1.

---

## Things NOT to do in this lesson

- **Don't oversell.** Don't promise outcomes. "I'm trying this; here's what it is" beats "this is going to be amazing."
- **Don't hide anything.** If a kid asks a question you don't know the answer to, say so. ("Good question — I don't know. I'll find out.")
- **Don't make AI sound magical.** It's a tool. It's not smart. It's a fast writer with patterns. Demystifying it builds trust faster than hyping it.
- **Don't compare to other tech the school uses.** "It's not like the bad kind of AI" sounds defensive. Just describe what this is.
- **Don't promise privacy you can't deliver.** Be honest about exactly what data the AI does and doesn't see. The privacy explainer in `setup/permissions/privacy-explainer.md` has the specifics.
- **Don't apologize for trying something.** Some kids will sense uncertainty. That's fine. Don't translate it into "sorry, I'm not sure about this." Translate it into "I'm trying this and I'll be honest about how it goes."
